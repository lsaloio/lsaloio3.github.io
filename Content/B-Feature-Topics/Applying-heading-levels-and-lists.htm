Applying heading levels and lists in MadCap Flare

Heading levels and lists break up the information in a way that is more readable and establishes some relationship between information. Formatting your information with heading levels and lists allows the screen reader to tell users what information is a heading, or what information is part of a list. Screen readers can even use structural elements like headings to navigate or "skim" documents

Heading levels and lists communicate a form of organization of the page’s content to people with disabilities that may not be able to interpret it visually. They provide a stable, consistent, and predictable structure, while also giving us information cues about what users are reading.

MadCap Flare allows you to apply both heading levels and lists to your content in order to maintain a digitally accessible website.

Applying heading levels in MadCap Flare

Applying heading levels is a great way to break up your content into digestible chunks and establish a hierarchical relationship between sections. If done correctly, heading levels play an important role in making your content accessible, especially to users with screen readers. The screen readers can read the heading levels aloud which orients readers and informs them of an associating relationship between one section and another, such as an overarching section and its subsections. The following steps explain how to apply headings 1, 2, and 3.

  1. Click on the HTML element that corresponds to the text you want to apply Heading 1 to. This text should be the highest ranking heading of your content. The HTML elements appear to the left of the text that they are associated with.

    • Note that selecting the HTML elements ensures that the whole element is selected. If you try selecting the text itself, you may run into an issue where you are missing the option to apply certain heading levels

  2. In the Styles section of the top menu, click the down arrow next to Style. A drop-down menu appears with heading level options h1, h2, h3, h4, h5, and h6.

  3. Select the h1 heading level option from the drop-down menu. The heading level is applied to the selected text.

  4. Repeat this for any headings that are at the same level.

  5. Click on the HTML element that corresponds to the text you want to apply Heading 2 to. This should be the second highest ranking heading that begins a subsection under your Heading 1 section. The HTML elements appear to the left of the text that they are associated with.

  6. In the Styles section of the top menu, click the down arrow next to Style. A drop-down menu appears with heading level options h1, h2, h3, h4, h5, and h6.

  7. Select the h2 heading level option from the drop-down menu. The heading level is applied to the selected text.

  8. Repeat this for any headings that are at the same level.

  9. Click on the HTML element that corresponds to the text you want to apply Heading 3 to. This should be the third highest ranking heading that begins a subsection under your Heading 2 section. The HTML elements appear to the left of the text that they are associated with.

  10. In the Styles section of the top menu, click the down arrow next to Style. A drop-down menu appears with heading level options h1, h2, h3, h4, h5, and h6.

  11. Select the h3 heading level option from the drop-down menu. The heading level is applied to the selected text.

  12. Repeat this for any headings that are at the same level.

  13. To apply any smaller heading levels, such as h4, h5, and h6, repeat the steps that you followed to apply headings levels h1, h2, and h3.

    • Note that using too many heading levels may make it more difficult for readers to follow significant connections between sections of information. This decreases the readability and accessibility of information since readers may become disoriented within too many heading levels

Applying lists in MadCap Flare

Like headings, lists are very important structurally because they inform users that items are related to each other in some way. For accessibility, lists allow users with screen readers to hear when content is in list form, therefore cuing them in on the relationship between items.

Creating Unordered and Ordered Lists

Starting a list before you have written any content

  1. Click on the space in your document that you want to begin a list item.

Starting a list after you have already written the content

  1. Select all of the text that you want to put into list form. Make sure that the HTML elements that correspond to each line are selected as well.

Creating unordered lists

  1. With the desired text selected, click on the down arrow next to the bullet list icon in the Paragraph section of the top-menu. A drop-down menu appears with different list options.

  2. Select Bullet List from the drop-down menu. The bullet list form is applied to your text.

    • Note: There are also Circle Bullet List and Square Bullet List alternative options to choose from in the drop-down menu.

Creating ordered lists

  1. With the desired text selected, click on the down arrow next to the bullet list icon in the Paragraph section of the top-menu. A drop-down menu appears with different list options.

  2. Select Numbered List from the drop-down menu, or choose one of the alternative Numbered List options. The numbered list form is applied to your text.

Creating description lists

  1. Select the text that contains the term you want to define.

  2. Click on the down arrow next to the bullet list icon in the Paragraph section of the top-menu. A drop-down menu appears with different list options.

  3. Select Definition List from the drop-down menu. The definition list is applied to your text, and two HTML elements appear to the left of your text called <dl> and <dt>. These mean that the definition list is applied and the text you selected is the definition term.

  4. With your cursor placed at the end of your term, press the Enter or Return key to start a new line. The line is automatically indented, and the HTML element <dd> appears on the left. This means that this space is for the definition component of the term.

  5. Type the definition of your term in the indented line.

Creating nested lists

Nested lists allow for a hierarchical structure within lists. Adding a nested list item shows that it is related to the listed line above it.

  1. Choose which listed item you would like to put a nested list item below.

  2. Click to place your cursor at the end of the chosen list item.

  3. Press the Enter or Return key to start a new list item.

  4. Press the Tab key to indent the list. This begins your nested list.

  5. Type your nested list item.
