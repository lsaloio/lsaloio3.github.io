  1. Heading levels and lists

But first, let's get into the why and who.

Why does digital accessibility matter?

In the fast-paced and innovative world of technology that we live in today, not being allowed to use technology with the same ease as everyone else would put anyone at an unfair disadvantage. People with disabilities continue hit barriers when they are not given the same convenience and ease of using technology everyone else. It is important to use digital accessibility guidelines and tactics so that people with disabilities are not excluded from the digital world that we have the opportunity to explore today.

Designing your digital content to be accessible also ensures that it will reach the most diverse audience possible. High quality websites and digital material need to be accessible so that no one is excluded from using their information, services, or products. This is especially important for developers, businesses, or organizations that are trying to make a business out of their digital content.

There are also accessibility laws and policies, specifically Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, that require companies and organizations to make their websites digitally accessible to all. WCAG, which stands for Web Content Accessibility Guidelines, is a useful set of guidelines from the World Wide Web Consortium that establishes different levels of conformance to web accessibility.

Who does digital accessibility affect?

Digital accessibility aims to include a wide range of disabilities that people may have, such as:

  • Auditory

  • Visual

  • Neurological

  • Cognitive

  • Physical

  • Speech

Digital accessibility also impacts people without disabilities. Consider these scenarios:

  • Being in an environment where you cannot listen to audio loudly or have too bright of a screen

  • Having bad internet connection

  • Temporary disabilities such as a broken arm or hand that impacts physical movement

  • Elderly people with evolving abilities

  • People with smaller or larger device screens that may need different settings

Each of these situations may not involve someone with a disability, but still require content to be digitally accessible.
